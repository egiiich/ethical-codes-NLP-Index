# Discussion

## Main findings

The results indicate that corporate codes of ethics in this sample are becoming more formalized over time, but the process is uneven and structurally imbalanced. The most consistent statistical signal is temporal: newer documents tend to achieve higher total ethics quality scores. This pattern appears in bivariate correlation and remains significant in the controlled OLS model with country and industry effects. Robustness checks largely support the same directional result: winsorization, outlier trimming, and bootstrap inference preserve a positive year effect. However, narrower specifications (such as a post-2015 subsample and median-split transformation) reduce significance. The most reasonable interpretation is therefore progressive but non-linear maturation of codified ethics, rather than a uniform year-by-year improvement.

A second core finding is asymmetry in how ethical obligations are distributed inside documents. Worker-to-company is the strongest principle domain in both mean intensity and prevalence, while worker-to-manager is consistently the weakest. This asymmetry is visible in the index matrix and mirrored at item level in the wide principle matrix, which strengthens confidence that the pattern is not an artifact of aggregation. The paired tests further show that these differences are not only cross-sectional; they exist within the same documents. In substantive terms, many codes strongly articulate what employees must do for the organization, but are less explicit about upward voice rights, challenge mechanisms, and protected escalation toward management.

A third finding concerns the contrast between procedural compliance growth and relational governance depth. Hotline-related content and the compliance institutionalization proxy increase over time, suggesting expansion of formal mechanisms. Yet company-to-worker growth is limited in key period comparisons, and vertical reciprocity principles remain sparse. This implies that organizations may be improving the procedural shell of ethics governance faster than its reciprocal social core. In other words, channels and frameworks are expanding, but the implied ethical contract between employees and managerial authority is less equally developed.

The country and industry analyses add contextual nuance. Country effects are statistically significant overall, and adjusted comparisons indicate higher scores for Japan and Korea relative to the China reference group in the baseline model. Industry differences are strong in unadjusted ANOVA but lose individual-coefficient significance under full controls. Together these results suggest that institutional context matters, yet observed differences are partly compositional and not reducible to fixed national or sectoral “ethics cultures.” Heterogeneity appears real, but it is mediated by sample structure, time distribution, and cross-category overlap.

Reliability diagnostics support the use of a composite total index. Cronbach’s alpha is above accepted thresholds, and principal-component concentration indicates meaningful shared variance among the eleven dimensions. This justifies the use of a summed quality proxy for inferential models, while still requiring domain-level interpretation because high and low principles are not distributed evenly. The combination of reliability and asymmetry findings therefore supports a dual reading: the composite is statistically coherent, yet substantively multidimensional.

The orientation test captures this multidimensionality directly. The company-interest composite significantly exceeds the employee-protection composite within documents, and the gap is large. This result is theoretically important because it reframes “quality” from simple quantity of ethical text toward directional balance of obligations and protections. A document can be extensive and still institutionally one-sided. In this dataset, higher codification often coexists with a governance structure centered on employee duties rather than reciprocal accountability. For a thesis focused on code quality, this is a central conclusion: quality is not only about how much is written, but about whose interests are structurally safeguarded by what is written.

## Possible connections to ESG controversies

The APAC controversy mapping provides a practical external lens for interpreting the matrix results in this thesis. Across the selected cases, the same governance weakness appears repeatedly: organizations often formalize compliance language faster than they institutionalize protected upward challenge and managerial accountability. This pattern closely matches the empirical orientation found in the corpus, where worker-to-company duties are typically stronger than worker-to-manager protections and reciprocal manager obligations.

In investor-governance controversies, the Luckin Coffee case illustrates how quickly trust collapses when investor-facing claims are not supported by reliable internal controls and escalation pathways. This maps directly to `company_to_investors`, `worker_to_manager`, and `hotline_principles`. The key implication for thesis interpretation is that disclosure sophistication alone is not a resilience indicator. A code can be extensive in external integrity language while remaining internally fragile if employees cannot escalate concerns safely and effectively.

The APAC governance-pressure cases (including Toshiba and Samsung-linked leadership accountability disputes) reinforce the same structural point from different institutional contexts. In both types of cases, formal policies are insufficient when managerial pressure discourages challenge behavior or weakens response quality. In matrix terms, this concentrates attention on `worker_to_manager`, `manager_to_worker`, `hotline_principles`, and `company_to_investors`. The interpretation for this thesis is that reciprocal governance capacity—not only procedural codification—should be treated as a core dimension of code quality.

Product integrity and customer-facing risk provide another strong connection. The Toyota-oriented safety and certification context shows how failures in upward and downward communication can become buyer-facing and public-safety problems. This aligns with the thesis finding that voice-related principles are often less dense than company-centered obligation language. The relevant domains are `worker_to_manager`, `manager_to_worker`, `company_to_buyer`, and `company_to_people`, suggesting that communication architecture inside the code is directly linked to operational risk containment.

The mapping also highlights external-interface risk concentrations that are especially relevant for APAC business models with dense cross-border production networks. Recurring supply-chain labor controversies show that weak supplier governance generates high-cost reputational and regulatory exposure. These patterns map to `company_to_supplier`, `company_to_people`, `hotline_principles`, and `worker_to_worker`. For interpretation of this corpus, supplier-facing principles should be considered part of core ethics infrastructure rather than peripheral CSR framing.

A related pressure point is ESG claim integrity in financial and corporate disclosure settings. Recent greenwashing and overstatement enforcement trends show that public sustainability language can create liability when evidence trails, ownership of controls, and escalation accountability are weak. This signal maps primarily to `company_to_investors` and `hotline_principles`, with spillover to `company_to_people` where social-impact claims are made. The implication is that high textual ambition in codes must be matched by verifiable implementation systems.

Taken together, the APAC controversy mapping supports a clear interpretation framework for the thesis: the highest-impact risks emerge where three weaknesses overlap—limited upward voice (`worker_to_manager`), procedural compliance without reciprocal management behavior (`hotline_principles` without strong `manager_to_worker`), and under-specified external-interface governance (`company_to_investors`, `company_to_supplier`, `company_to_people`, `company_to_buyer`). This does not establish direct causality for firms in the dataset, but it substantially strengthens external plausibility by showing that the lowest-balance areas in the matrix correspond to recurrent controversy mechanisms in the region.

## How the methodology can be used in scientific literature

The methodology contributes to scientific literature primarily through its measurement architecture. By combining an item-level presence matrix with a domain-level index matrix, it provides transparent traceability between coding decisions and aggregate scores. This is valuable because many ethics-governance studies rely on broad disclosure indicators with limited visibility into how constructs are built. In this framework, researchers can inspect whether aggregate findings are consistent with micro-level coding prevalence, which strengthens construct validity and supports replicability.

A second contribution is inferential structure. The study does not rely on a single model but uses layered evidence: descriptives, paired within-document tests, omnibus group comparisons, multivariate regressions with robust standard errors, reliability diagnostics, and sensitivity checks. This hierarchy can serve as a reusable template in future studies of corporate ethics text. It distinguishes between different kinds of claims—association, conditional difference, orientation imbalance, and robustness—thereby reducing overinterpretation from any one technique.

A third contribution is conceptual granularity. The framework operationalizes ethics-code quality as multidimensional relational governance rather than binary adoption. This enables hypothesis testing on directional patterns, such as company-interest orientation versus employee-protection orientation, and on structural components like compliance institutionalization. Such granularity can move scholarship beyond “does a code exist?” toward “what governance logic does the code embed?” That shift is important for theory development in business ethics, corporate governance, and organizational institutionalism.

The method is also suitable for integration with controversy and policy-shock research designs. Even in its current form, the synthesis explores temporal alignment between major ESG events and domain-level shifts. Future studies could formalize this through panel designs, lagged event models, or quasi-experimental strategies where repeated firm documents are available. The existing variables (year, country, industry, multidomain scores) provide a practical base for these extensions.

Another research pathway concerns latent-structure modeling. Reliability results suggest common variance, but substantial domain heterogeneity remains. This invites future use of confirmatory factor analysis, item-response approaches, or hierarchical models to test whether higher-order ethics governance capacity can be decomposed into subfactors such as procedural compliance, stakeholder integrity, and voice reciprocity. Because the current dataset preserves both micro and macro coding levels, it is well suited for such methodological development.

Interdisciplinary applicability is another advantage. Legal scholars can analyze obligation asymmetry and rights articulation; organizational behavior researchers can link voice-related domains to psychological safety proxies; finance and ESG researchers can connect investor-facing domains to disclosure risk and enforcement outcomes. The shared coding language across stakeholders makes the framework portable, while the explicit R workflow supports reproducibility and cumulative comparison across studies.

At the same time, scientific use should keep a clear epistemic boundary: these indices measure codified intent and formalization, not realized conduct. The strongest literature contribution will likely emerge when this methodology is combined with external outcomes such as litigation, sanctions, whistleblowing dynamics, or employee survey signals. In that combined form, the framework can help explain not only how firms describe ethics, but when and why those descriptions align—or fail to align—with behavior.

## How the methodology can be applied in practice in management

In management practice, the methodology can function as a structured governance diagnostic. By scoring current code documents across the same domains, organizations can identify where their ethics architecture is concentrated and where it is underdeveloped. This is especially useful for moving internal discussions away from generic claims (“our code is comprehensive”) toward evidence-based design questions (“which relational obligations are under-specified?”).

A direct application is risk-based revision planning. The domain logic allows management to prioritize updates according to sector exposure. Supply-chain-intensive firms can emphasize supplier and buyer principles; financial institutions can focus on investor-communication integrity and claim-evidence consistency; high-impact industries can strengthen people, community, and escalation domains. Instead of revising entire documents uniformly, teams can allocate effort where risk materiality is highest.

The framework is also useful for aligning policy text with implementation systems. The thesis findings suggest that hotline architecture may grow faster than reciprocal managerial-accountability language. In practice, this signals a need to pair document revision with managerial KPIs, non-retaliation controls, investigation standards, and feedback loops to employees. Without this alignment, increased procedural text may produce only symbolic assurance.

Board governance is another practical domain. A matrix-based dashboard can show year-on-year movement in total score and domain balance, allowing boards and audit committees to challenge one-dimensional progress narratives. If company-interest domains rise while employee-protection domains stagnate, oversight bodies can request corrective action before weak reciprocity becomes a governance liability.

The methodology can also support due diligence in partnerships, procurement ecosystems, and transactions. A standardized domain profile enables faster identification of potential governance blind spots in counterparties. This does not replace full legal or operational diligence, but it helps triage where deeper investigation is needed.

Finally, the approach can be institutionalized as an annual policy cycle. Organizations can set dual targets: increase overall maturity and reduce directional imbalance between obligation and protection composites. This guards against the common pattern in which policy volume expands while core trust mechanisms remain unchanged. If implemented cross-functionally—compliance, legal, HR, sustainability, procurement, internal audit—the framework can become an ongoing control instrument rather than a one-off assessment.

## Limitations of the methodology

The most important limitation is ontological: the method evaluates documents, not behavior. High scores indicate codified coverage and intensity, but they cannot verify whether principles are implemented in daily decisions. Conversely, modest scores do not necessarily imply weak ethical culture if strong informal norms exist. Therefore, conclusions should be framed as assessments of formal governance architecture.

A second limitation is data structure. The sample is not a fully balanced longitudinal panel at firm level, and year-country-industry distributions are uneven. This constrains causal claims about improvement over time and about event-driven changes. Some observed trends may partly reflect shifts in sample composition rather than within-firm evolution.

Third, metadata quality affects inference. The synthesis notes industry-label inconsistencies and sparse observations in some categories and years. These issues can weaken coefficient precision, inflate uncertainty in subgroup comparisons, and complicate interpretation of sector effects.

Fourth, coding still involves interpretive judgment. Even with clear categories, differences in document language, translation, and drafting style can influence whether an item is coded as absent, brief mention, or explicit section. The two-matrix design improves transparency, but inter-coder reliability procedures and calibration reporting would further strengthen methodological rigor.

Fifth, aggregation choices simplify reality. The unweighted total index is useful for comparability, yet principle domains are unlikely to have equal practical significance across sectors. A one-point increase in supplier governance may be more material in one industry than in another. Future applications could test sector-adjusted weighting schemes while preserving baseline comparability.

Sixth, model form may underfit complexity. OLS with robust errors offers interpretability, but relationships may involve nonlinearities, interactions, and threshold effects. Additional modeling strategies could capture heterogeneous year effects or context-specific dynamics more accurately.

Seventh, controversy linkage in this project is interpretive rather than causal. Temporal consistency between events and coding shifts is informative, but attribution remains uncertain because regulatory changes, investor pressure, peer effects, and internal reforms often occur simultaneously.

Eighth, external validity is bounded by corpus composition. The observed countries and industries provide meaningful variation, but results should not be generalized uncritically to all corporate environments, especially where disclosure norms, legal systems, or document practices differ substantially.

In sum, the methodology offers a robust and replicable way to assess the structural quality of ethics codes, particularly their directional balance and domain coverage. Its greatest value is diagnostic clarity; its main constraint is that textual quality is an intermediate indicator, not direct proof of ethical outcomes. Future work that links this framework to behavioral and enforcement data can substantially increase explanatory power while retaining the methodological strengths demonstrated here.

An additional implication for both scholarship and practice is the possibility of using directional imbalance as an early governance stress indicator. The thesis results show that organizations often codify compliance controls and employee duties more extensively than reciprocal protection and upward voice. Future research can test whether this imbalance predicts subsequent controversy incidence more strongly than total score alone. If confirmed, this would shift the field from aggregate maturity metrics toward structural fragility metrics, where the key question is not only how much ethics text exists, but whether the architecture can surface inconvenient information before harm escalates.

For managers, the same logic suggests that ethics-code revision should be sequenced as a change program rather than a drafting exercise. In many organizations, legal and compliance teams can update policy language quickly, while incentive redesign, managerial capability building, and investigation quality controls take longer. The matrix can therefore be used as a transition map: first identify asymmetries, then define implementation milestones that close them over several reporting cycles. This temporal sequencing is important because a visible mismatch between ambitious policy claims and slow operational change can itself create reputational risk. In this sense, the methodology is not only an assessment instrument but also a governance planning tool that helps organizations align document architecture, management behavior, and external accountability narratives over time.
